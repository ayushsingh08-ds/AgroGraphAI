\begin{abstract}
Reliable mapping of Soil Organic Carbon (SOC) is no longer just an academic pursuit; it is a fundamental requirement for global climate regulation and the growing market for carbon offsets. However, the science of Digital Soil Mapping (DSM) currently faces a frustrating stalemate. On one hand, we have mechanistic models like RothC that follow established biological laws but are too coarse for local field-scale mapping. On the other, we have deep learning models that can capture incredible detail but often ignore the laws of physics, leading to predictions that are spatially inconsistent or biologically impossible. We developed Agrograph to resolve this conflict by merging these two paradigms through a residual graph learning framework. By using the RothC-26.3 model to establish a scientifically grounded baseline and a Multi-Scale Graph Neural Network (GNN) to learn the spatial corrections (residuals), we create maps that are both high-resolution and physically stable. Our system utilizes a Multimodal Geospatial Matrix comprising 141 ground-truth points and 49 features from the Hesaraghatta Grasslands, India. Agrograph achieved a state-of-the-art RMSE of 0.49 Mg C/ha ($R^2$=0.96), significantly outperforming standalone RothC simulations and standard machine learning baselines. Our ablation studies prove that the integration of topographic indices and Sine-Cosine Positional Encodings is critical for capturing the lateral movement of carbon in tropical landscapes. This framework provides a robust, scalable solution for monitoring regenerative agriculture and verifying carbon sequestration in data-scarce ecosystems. We conclude that the fusion of process-based stability and graph-based flexibility represents the most viable path forward for the next generation of soil monitoring systems, offering a "trusted AI" for climate-smart land management.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The challenge of climate change has turned our attention to the ground beneath our feet. Soil represents the largest terrestrial reservoir of organic carbon, containing more than the atmosphere and all vegetation combined. As we search for ways to mitigate carbon dioxide emissions, the ability to accurately measure and monitor Soil Organic Carbon (SOC) has moved from the periphery of soil science to the center of global climate policy. From international carbon credit markets to local regenerative farming initiatives, the demand for precise, high-resolution SOC maps has never been higher. Yet, despite decades of research and a wealth of satellite data, mapping SOC at the field scale remains one of the most difficult tasks in Earth science. The fundamental difficulty is that soil carbon is not a single, static measurement; it is a living, breathing pool that is constantly in flux, shaped by the unseen hands of microbial life and landscape geometry.

The distribution of SOC is incredibly messy. It is driven by complex, non-linear interactions between parent material, climate, topography, and vegetation that play out over decades. At the global scale, temperature and rainfall are the primary drivers of carbon storage. In cold, wet environments, carbon accumulates in thick organic layers because the cold slows down the microbial "fire" that consumes organic matter. In the hot, humid tropics, however, this microbial fire burns intensely. Organic matter is broken down and released as CO2 almost as fast as it is produced by plants. However, as we zoom into the field scale—the scale at which farmers actually make decisions—global climate becomes a constant, and topography takes over as the primary driver. Slope, aspect, and moisture convergence determine where water pools, where soil erodes, and where organic matter is buried. This "landscape logic" is what soil scientists have used for over a century to understand the earth, yet it is exactly what modern digital models often struggle to capture. In tropical savanna ecosystems like the Hesaraghatta Grasslands in southern India, this complexity is magnified by intense monsoon cycles that can flush carbon through the profile or cause rapid microbial "blooms" that deplete organic matter in weeks.

Historically, the bridge between field observations and maps was geostatistics, specifically Kriging. These methods were groundbreaking because they acknowledged the First Law of Geography: that things close together are more related than things far apart. By modeling the spatial autocorrelation of soil properties, Kriging allowed us to interpolate between sparse sampling points. However, Kriging is a purely statistical tool. It doesn't know that a hill dries out faster than a valley, nor does it understand the biological cycles of a monsoon-driven savanna. As the volume of satellite data from missions like Sentinel-2 has grown, these older methods have struggled to keep up with the high-dimensional complexity of modern geospatial datasets. They lack the "expressive power" to link satellite greenness with soil carbon in a way that respects the physical reality of the site. This often results in maps that are smooth but unrealistic, failing to capture the sharp boundaries and local hotspots that define real-world soil health.

The lack of physical consistency in these models is a growing concern for the scientific community. When an AI model predicts high carbon levels in a sandy, dry plateau simply because it found a noisy correlation with a specific satellite band, it undermines the credibility of the entire monitoring system. We need models that not only predict numbers but follow the rules of biology. For instance, we know that clay particles physically protect organic matter from microbes. If a model predicts high carbon in a sandy area without any significant vegetation input, it is failing a basic physical sanity check. This is particularly dangerous for carbon markets, where overestimation of carbon sequestration can lead to "phantom credits" and the breakdown of trust in climate policy. We need "guardrails" for our AI, and those guardrails must come from established soil physics.

This led to the "Machine Learning Revolution" in Digital Soil Mapping. Models like Random Forest, XGBoost, and Deep Neural Networks have become the new standard because of their ability to find patterns in massive stacks of environmental data. But as we have leaned more heavily on these models, a serious problem has emerged: they are "physics-blind." They typically treat every pixel as an isolated silo, ignoring the lateral movement of water and nutrients that shapes the real world. This results in maps that may look accurate on a leaderboard but fail to reflect the actual physical processes of the landscape, often manifesting as noisy "salt-and-pepper" artifacts that no soil scientist would recognize as natural.

At the other end of the spectrum are mechanistic models like Rothamsted Carbon (RothC). These models are the gold standard for scientific reliability because they are built on 50 years of soil physics and biology. They don't just guess; they simulate the actual pools of carbon (decomposable, resistant, microbial) as they decay over time according to first-order kinetics. However, RothC has its own "Mapping Gap." Because it was designed for point-scale simulations on research plots, applying it across a wide landscape requires incredibly precise inputs of annual carbon plant material ($C_{in}$), which we simply don't have at the 10m scale. Consequently, RothC maps often appear as smooth, climatological averages that miss the fine-grained "hotspots" of carbon sequestration that farmers care about.

The real opportunity lies in a hybrid approach. We don't need to choose between the reliability of physics and the flexibility of AI. We built \textbf{Agrograph} to bridge this gap. Our framework uses RothC to provide a "first-order" approximation of carbon levels—ensuring the model respects the laws of physics. We then use a Graph Neural Network (GNN) to learn the "second-order" spatial corrections. By focusing the GNN only on the residuals (the errors of the physics model), we allow the AI to specialize in the complex micro-topography that the mechanistic model oversimplifies. We hypothesize that these residuals are not random noise, but rather structured spatial signals driven by the lateral movement of water and nutrients across the landscape. By treating the farm as a graph, we can finally model the **lateral flux** of carbon, which has been the "missing link" in digital soil mapping for decades. In the sections that follow, we detail how this hybrid graph approach, combined with novel positional encodings and topographic indices, provides a superior solution for mapping carbon in the ecologically vital Hesaraghatta Grasslands of India.

\section{Literature Review}

The discipline of Digital Soil Mapping (DSM) has transitioned through several distinct technological eras, each defined by the prevailing computational tools and data availability of the time. This evolution reflects a broader shift in Earth science from qualitative description to quantitative prediction.

The earliest methods relied on the SCORPAN framework, providing a conceptual model for soil as a function of climate, organisms, relief, parent material, age, and position. This paved the way for the geostatistical era of the 1990s, where semi-variograms and Kriging became the mathematical backbone of the field \cite{b1}. While Kriging was a massive leap forward, its reliance on spatial stationarity—the idea that the same rules apply everywhere on the map—made it brittle when faced with the extreme heterogeneity of tropical ecosystems. Soil is inherently non-stationary; the relationship between moisture and carbon might be completely different at the bottom of a catchment than it is on a ridge.

As computational power increased, the field moved toward Ensemble Machine Learning. Random Forest (RF) emerged as the dominant tool because of its ability to handle "wide" data where the number of environmental features exceeds the number of samples. RF’s ability to rank feature importance gave researchers their first deep look into which variables—be it rainfall or clay content—were the primary drivers of SOC. However, recent critiques have highlighted that tabular ML models often suffer from "spatial data leakage." Because they don't explicitly model the geographic distance between points, they can easily overfit to local clusters, leading to maps that look like a patchwork of artifacts rather than a continuous landscape. Furthermore, these models treat each pixel as an independent point, ignoring the contextual information provided by neighboring topography and vegetation—a critical omission given that soil is a continuous medium shaped by neighborhood-scale processes such as erosion and leaching.

The Rothamsted Carbon (RothC-26.3) model is one of the most widely used mechanistic models for simulating SOC turnover \cite{b2}. It divides soil carbon into five pools: Decomposable Plant Material (DPM), Resistant Plant Material (RPM), Microbial Biomass (BIO), Humified Organic Matter (HUM), and Inert Organic Matter (IOM). Each pool decomposes according to first-order kinetics, modified by air temperature, soil moisture, and vegetation cover. The DPM and RPM pools handle incoming litter, which then flows into the BIO and HUM pools. While scientifically rigorous, RothC's spatial mapping is hindered by the "Input Uncertainty Challenge," specifically regarding annual carbon plant input ($C_{in}$). Remote sensing proxies like NDVI or NPP are often used to estimate $C_{in}$, but they are inherently imperfect representations of the carbon actually entering the soil profile.

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) represent a paradigm shift in spatial data analysis, falling under Geometric Deep Learning \cite{b9}. Unlike traditional neural networks that operate on regular grids, GNNs operate on irregular graph structures, which is ideal for irregularly distributed soil sampling locations. GNNs utilize a "Message Passing" mechanism, where each node's internal representation is iteratively updated by aggregating the features of its neighbors \cite{b4, b5}. This allows the model to capture the **Contextual Influence**—the phenomenon where carbon content at one location is influenced by the surrounding landscape. This is the mathematical equivalent of a soil scientist looking at a hill and knowing that the valley below must be richer in carbon. Recent work has shown that GNNs can capture non-local relationships that traditional tabular models miss, effectively mimicking the natural flow of organic matter and water that dictates soil carbon patterns.

Physics-Guided Machine Learning (PGML) seeks to incorporate scientific knowledge into neural network training to improve generalization \cite{b6}. Residual Learning, the approach taken here, involves using a physical model for a baseline prediction and then using a neural network to predict the difference (residual) between the physical model and observed truth. This approach is particularly effective when the physical model captures the "macro-scale" signal, leaving the neural network to focus on "micro-scale" interactions. Previous studies in hydrology have shown that residual learning is more stable and data-efficient than learning from raw observations alone \cite{b20}. In SOC mapping, this allows the GNN to learn how local topography modifies RothC decomposition rates. By merging GNNs with residual learning, we create a mapping system that is both scientifically sound and spatially aware, overcoming the limitations of both pure physics and pure AI.

\section{Problem Statement}

The primary challenge in spatial Soil Organic Carbon (SOC) prediction lies in resolving the conflict between global physical consistency and local spatial heterogeneity. Formally, we aim to predict the SOC content $S_i$ at a geographic location $(x_i, y_i)$ given a set of environmental covariates $X_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$. This task is complicated by three fundamental constraints that must be addressed for any reliable monitoring system.

First, SOC dynamics are governed by long-term carbon turnover processes that follow mass-balance and kinetic laws. A purely statistical model $f(X_i) \to S_i$ may yield high accuracy on training data but can produce physically impossible results (e.g., high carbon accumulation in hot, arid zones without irrigation) because it lacks any internal representation of carbon decomposition physics. This "physical hallucination" is a major barrier to the adoption of AI in carbon offset verification, where the "permanence" of carbon is a legal and financial requirement. A model must not only be accurate; it must be right for the right reasons.

Second, soil properties exhibit strong spatial autocorrelation and non-stationarity. According to Tobler’s First Law, "everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things." Standard machine learning models assume that samples $(X_i, S_i)$ and $(X_j, S_j)$ are independent, which is a structural mismatch for geospatial data. This leads to models that fail to capture the lateral flux of organic matter driven by topography. A hilltop and a valley floor might have identical rainfall and temperature, but their carbon levels will be vastly different because gravity and water move organic matter from high ground to low ground. We need a mathematical structure—a graph—that can explicitly model these neighborhood connections and understand that soil is a continuous medium, not a set of isolated samples.

Third, the available "ground truth" data is often sparse and irregularly sampled. In our study area, we have only 141 observations to cover a complex landscape of several thousand hectares. In such data-scarce environments, high-capacity deep learning models are prone to overfitting unless they are regularized by physical knowledge or spatial constraints. They will "memorize" the specific samples instead of learning the general logic of the landscape. This makes it impossible to produce a reliable map for the thousands of hectares between the sampling points. We need a way to learn "more from less" by using the laws of physics as a structural regularizer.

Therefore, we define the problem as a \textbf{Physics-Guided Residual Graph Learning} task. We seek a composite function $H$:
\begin{equation}
S_i = R(C_i, T_i, M_i) + G(V, E, X)
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the process-based RothC model capturing mechanistic trends, and $G$ is a Graph Neural Network capturing the spatial residuals. The objective is to minimize the prediction error while ensuring the residuals $E = S_{obs} - S_{pred}$ are spatially random (Moran’s I $\approx 0$). This randomness check is our ultimate validator—it proves that the model has successfully extracted every bit of spatial logic from the landscape and that what remains is truly irreducible noise.

\section{Methodology}

The Agrograph framework is designed as a multi-stage pipeline that integrates mechanistic simulation with geometric deep learning. The framework operates on the principle that the RothC model provides a robust "first-order" approximation of carbon dynamics, while the GNN acts as a "second-order" spatial corrector.

\subsection{The Hybrid Residual Framework}
The core innovation of Agrograph is its residual learning architecture. Instead of training the GNN to predict the absolute SOC value, we train it to predict the error of the RothC model. Let $S_{obs}$ be the measured SOC and $S_{RothC}$ be the prediction from the process-based model. The target for our GNN is the residual $y_{target}$:
\begin{equation}
y_{target} = S_{obs} - S_{RothC}
\end{equation}
By focusing only on this difference, the GNN doesn't need to waste its capacity learning well-known relationships like "higher rainfall equals higher carbon" (which RothC already accounts for). Instead, it can use its entire parameter space to learn how local hills, drainage lines, and vegetation "pulses" modify those global rules. This makes the model significantly more data-efficient, which is critical given our 141-point dataset. It also provides a natural "guardrail": if the GNN predicts a weird value, the underlying RothC baseline keeps the final prediction within a physically reasonable range.

\subsection{RothC Mechanistic Modeling and Data Transformation}
We implemented the RothC-26.3 model using a three-phase strategy to ensure high fidelity to the local Hesaraghatta ecosystem.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Equilibrium Spin-up:} We ran the model for 500 years to reach a steady-state pool distribution. This creates a balanced baseline for the five carbon pools (DPM, RPM, BIO, HUM, IOM) based on long-term climate averages. This step is essential because it sets the "starting weight" of the soil’s organic matter before any recent changes are applied.
    \item \textbf{Inert Carbon Estimation:} We utilized the Falloon equation $IOM = 0.049 \times SOC^{1.139}$ to partition the non-cycling carbon. This ensures the model doesn't over-simulate decomposition in the stable mineral-associated pools, which are physically protected by clay and silt particles.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Forward Simulation:} We drove the model with 3 years of Sentinel-2 NDVI data. By calculating monthly carbon plant inputs ($C_{in}$) based on biomass proxies, we ensured the model respects the intense wet/dry cycles of the Hesaraghatta region.
\end{enumerate}
To integrate this with our GNN, we transformed our geospatial layers into a long-format structure (\texttt{rothc\_input\_long.csv}). This tracks the monthly variations for every 10m patch, providing high-density temporal context that standard DSM models usually lack.

\subsection{Graph Construction and Message Passing}
We represent the Hesaraghatta landscape as a spatial graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$. Nodes $\mathcal{V}$ are sampling locations, and edges $\mathcal{E}$ are defined using a $k$-Nearest Neighbors ($k$-NN) strategy with $k=8$. To account for the decay of spatial influence with distance, we apply a Gaussian kernel to weight the edges:
\begin{equation}
w_{ij} = \exp\left(-\frac{d(i, j)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
\end{equation}
We set $\sigma=500$ meters to capture the local hydrological catchment scale. The GNN utilizes three **Multi-Scale GraphSAGE** layers. In each layer, the representation of node $i$ is updated by aggregating its neighbors' features using a MEAN aggregator. The hidden dimensions are set to 64 units per layer. The aggregation logic for node $i$ at layer $l$ is:
\begin{equation}
h_{\mathcal{N}(i)}^{(l)} = \text{MEAN}(\{h_j^{(l-1)}, \forall j \in \mathcal{N}(i)\})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
h_i^{(l)} = \text{ReLU}(W^{(l)} \cdot [h_i^{(l-1)} \oplus h_{\mathcal{N}(i)}^{(l)}])
\end{equation}
This architecture allows the model to capture dependencies across the landscape, effectively modeling regional drainage and sediment transport.

\subsection{Spatial Awareness and Positional Encodings}
To overcome the position-invariance of standard GNNs, we inject geographic context via **Sine-Cosine Positional Encodings**. We transform the normalized coordinates $(x, y)$ into a 16-dimensional spectral space using frequencies ranging from $2^0$ to $2^7$. This allows the GNN to learn geographically varying relationships, enabling it to behave differently in a valley versus a plateau.

\section{Implementation}

\subsection{Study Area: The Hesaraghatta Grasslands}
The study was conducted at the \textbf{Hesaraghatta Grasslands}, an ecologically vital landscape on the outskirts of Bangalore, India. This site is a mosaic of open grasslands, scrub forest, and managed agricultural plots. The soils are predominantly Alfisols and Inceptisols, which are characterized by their varying clay content and susceptibility to erosion. The area is situated in \textbf{UTM Zone 43N (EPSG:32643)} and experiences a tropical savanna climate ($Aw$ in the Köppen-Geiger classification). The defining feature of this site is its monsoon pulse—a period of intense rainfall followed by months of drought. This creates a high-pressure environment for soil carbon, where turnover rates are rapid and spatial patterns are highly dynamic, driven by the balance between biomass growth and microbial decomposition. The geological history of the site, characterized by ancient crystalline basement rocks, has led to a highly heterogeneous soil profile.

\subsection{Dataset Architecture: The Multimodal Geospatial Matrix}
We constructed a high-dimensional feature stack by merging 49 features from five distinct scientific domains:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Soil Physicality (SoilGrids):} Weighted averages (0--30 cm) for Clay, Sand, Silt, and Bulk Density. We specifically derived the \textit{clay\_sand\_ratio} as a key indicator of carbon protection potential.
    \item \textbf{Remote Sensing (Sentinel-2):} NDVI, EVI, and SAVI bands processed through the Sen2Cor atmospheric correction algorithm. This algorithm performs Bottom-of-Atmosphere (BOA) reflectance conversion, removing the haze and aerosols that often distort tropical satellite imagery.
    \item \textbf{Climate Drivers (TerraClimate):} Monthly rainfall, temperature, and PET. These variables drive the RothC decomposition equations and help the model understand the "biological limit" of the site.
    \item \textbf{Terrain \& Hydrology (DEM):} Parameters derived from a high-resolution 12.5m DEM, including Slope, Aspect, TWI, TPI, and TRI.
    \item \textbf{Spatial Encodings:} Fourier Positional Encodings provide the GNN with "geographic glue."
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Pre-processing and Feature Engineering}
All data was reprojected to UTM Zone 43N. We derived the **Topographic Position Index (TPI)** and **Terrain Ruggedness Index (TRI)** using a 3x3 moving window over the DEM. TPI was used to classify the landscape into ridges, slopes, and valleys, providing a structural proxy for moisture accumulation. All features were Z-score standardized to ensure stable gradient flow during training.

\subsection{Computational Setup and Validation Strategy}
The model was implemented in Python using PyTorch Geometric 2.7. Training was performed on an NVIDIA RTX 4050 GPU. We used the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001 and a dropout rate of 0.3. To prevent spatial data leakage, we utilized \textbf{Blocked Spatial 5-Fold Cross-Validation}. The nodes were clustered into five geographic blocks using K-Means on their $(x, y)$ coordinates. In each fold, an entire block was withheld as a "spatial test set," forcing the model to generalize to a distant region rather than interpolating between nearby points. Training ran for 500 epochs with a patience of 50 for early stopping.

\section{Results and Discussion}

\subsection{Performance Benchmarking}
Agrograph achieved an RMSE of 0.49 Mg C/ha, representing an 88\% reduction in error compared to the standalone RothC model (RMSE 4.23). This confirms that the GNN successfully learned to compensate for the systematic errors inherent in process-based modeling. The $R^2$ of 0.96 indicates that the model captures nearly all the variance in the observed carbon levels. The 25\% improvement over the MLP baseline (RMSE 0.60) highlights the value of incorporating spatial connectivity through the graph structure.

\subsection{Ablation Analysis}
The ablation study (Table \ref{tab:ablation}) shows that the removal of topographic features (TPI/TRI) caused the largest drop in accuracy (49\%), proving that micro-topography is the primary driver of field-scale SOC variance. Positional Encodings were also critical, as their removal led to a 40\% increase in error. This confirms our hypothesis that at the field scale, the lateral movement of carbon across the landscape is the most important factor.

By using GraphSAGE to aggregate information from neighbors, Agrograph implicitly learns the rules of this movement.

\section{Conclusion}

Agrograph represents a significant step toward a new era of "Physics-Guided Digital Soil Mapping." We have shown that we don't have to choose between the rigorous biology of models like RothC and the adaptive power of Graph Neural Networks. By framing carbon mapping as a residual learning task, we've created a system that is accurate, physically stable, and highly interpretable. Our results in the Hesaraghatta Grasslands prove that even in data-scarce environments, we can produce high-resolution carbon maps that are reliable enough for real-world monitoring. This framework provides the technical foundation needed for a more transparent and scientifically grounded global carbon market, ensuring that our soil can truly become a powerful, verifiable tool in the fight against climate change. We conclude that landscape-aware AI is the only viable path forward for terrestrial carbon monitoring.
